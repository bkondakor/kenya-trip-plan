About Mount Kenya

Mount Kenya, rising to 5,199 meters (17,057 feet) at its highest peak Batian, is Africa's second-highest mountain and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. This ancient volcano, once taller than Kilimanjaro, has been sculpted by millions of years of glacial erosion into a spectacular alpine landscape featuring jagged peaks, hanging valleys, glacial lakes, and unique afro-alpine vegetation found nowhere else on Earth.

The mountain is sacred to Kenya's Kikuyu people, who traditionally believed their supreme deity Ngai lived on the summit. The name "Kenya" itself derives from "Kirinyaga," meaning "mountain of whiteness" in Kikuyu, referring to the glaciers that crown the peaks. Today, Mount Kenya National Park protects this extraordinary ecosystem, which features 11 distinct vegetation zones from lowland forest through bamboo, heath, moorland, and finally to the glacial peaks.

While the twin peaks of Batian and Nelion require technical rock climbing, Point Lenana (4,985m) can be reached by trekkers without climbing experience, making Mount Kenya accessible to adventurers of all levels. The most popular routes - Sirimon, Naro Moru, and Chogoria - each offer unique perspectives on this magnificent mountain. Beyond trekking, the mountain's lower slopes harbor elephants, buffaloes, giant forest hogs, and the rare bongo antelope, while the peaks host rock hyrax, leopards, and diverse high-altitude bird species.

üìÖ Best Time to Visit

January - February & July - October

Dry seasons offer the best trekking conditions with clearer skies and better summit visibility. December - March are slightly warmer. Avoid April - May (long rains) and November (short rains) when trails can be muddy and views obscured.

Point Lenana Summit Trek

Reach Point Lenana (4,985m) via Sirimon, Naro Moru, or Chogoria routes. 4-5 day treks through stunning alpine landscapes to stand atop Africa's second-highest trekking peak.

Technical Climbing

For experienced climbers, tackle the technical rock routes to Batian (5,199m) and Nelion (5,188m) peaks, featuring classic multi-pitch alpine climbing.

Afro-Alpine Ecosystem

Witness extraordinary giant groundsels and lobelias found only in East African high-altitude zones. Experience 11 distinct vegetation zones from montane forest to glaciers.

Glaciers and Alpine Lakes

Visit the remaining equatorial glaciers (Lewis, Tyndall, Darwin) and pristine alpine tarns including Lake Michaelson and the Emerald Lakes.

Wildlife Encounters

Spot elephants, buffaloes, and rare bongo antelope in lower forests. See rock hyrax, mountain reedbuck, and duiker at higher altitudes.

High-Altitude Bird Watching

Over 130 bird species including scarlet-tufted malachite sunbirds, Mackinder's eagle owls, and the endemic Mount Kenya francolin.

Where to Stay
